String Instruments

These instruments produce sound through the vibration of strings, which can be plucked, bowed, or struck

  1. Violin
  2. Guitar
  3. Cello
  4. Double Bass
  5. Banjo
  6. Sitar

VIOLIN

The violin is a string instrument typically tuned to the notes G, D, A, and E.
It is played with a bow (made of horsehair) that is drawn across the strings, or by plucking the strings (pizzicato).
The pitch is controlled by pressing the strings against the fingerboard.

GUITAR

The guitar is a versatile stringed instrument, typically with six strings, played by strumming or plucking.
It comes in various forms, including acoustic, electric, and bass.
Widely used across genres, it has a rich history and is a cornerstone of modern music.

CELLO

The cello is a string instrument in the violin family that's played with a bow or by plucking the strings.
It's known for its rich, resonant tones and is often used in orchestral compositions and solo performence

DOULE BASS

The double bass is the largest string instrument in the orchestra, and is also known as the contrabass, upright bass, acoustic bass, or just "the bass".
The double bass produces low, muted, and powerful tones that often form a stable foundation for the rest of the orchestra

BANJO

The banjo was created by enslaved Africans and their descendants in the Caribbean and colonial North America. The instrument was a combination of West African and European forms
The banjo has a drum-like body made from a gourd or calabash, a neck, and strings. The earliest banjos had four gut strings, but the standard modern banjo has five metal strings

SITAR

The sitar originated in the Indian subcontinent in the 18th century. It's believed to have been developed by Khusrau Khan, a figure of the Mughal Empire, from the Iranian instrument, the setar.
The sitar is known for its unique sound, which comes from its two layers of strings and deep, round chamber.
